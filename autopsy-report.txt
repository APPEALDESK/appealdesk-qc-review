What is a Persuasion Pattern?
It's an effective persuasion device uncovered in the autopsy that can be used in any niche to boost the selling power of your copy. There are instructions on how to use each device below accompanied by dissected examples from the autopsy.
How do I use these patterns?
Think of this as a toolbox for increasing the persuasiveness of your copy. You can go through it like a checklist, looking for ideas to ramp up your conversions in just about any marketing promotion. Also, as an exercise to help cement these ideas and principles, I'd suggest going through each one or choosing particular patterns of your choice and applying them in either a real or mock situation. Ask yourself, " how can I or would I use this right now?" Ł it's that type of thinking that will make this material stick and have you thinking like a master-level copywriter.
Pattern #1 : Assumptive Phrase Mastery (5 Different Types).
You've likely heard of the popular phrase, "assume the sale", b ut how do you apply that to sales copy? In the examples below, you'll find five different examples of how Dan uses the words " you will" or "you'll" so that whatever comes after it, will feel certain, like it's bound to happen, which is how you want prospects to feel about buying and all of the necessary steps in-between.

This structure is very powerful in that you're having them agree to something about themselves first and then stating that because of that, they will do or agree to something else.

In this variation, you're starting with a hypothetical situation and drawing a conclusion that feels like it would be certain, yet it's really still hypothetical. Pattern #2 : Using The Power of "3".
Recognize these popular stories? T he Three Little Pigs, Three Musketeers, Goldilocks and The Three bears, Three Blind Mice, Three Wise Men. Or how about these concepts: ABC-123. The Father, the Son and the Holy Spirit. Blood, sweat and tears. The good, the bad, and the ugly. On your mark, get set, go! No doubt, the number three is a major, universal theme in our society, but why? My take: it has to do with the number three's sense of completion. To show you what I mean, let's look at its surrounding numbers. When we think of the number two, we often relate it to a matchup or a duo. If you have four things to share, it starts to feel like a list. And if you have five points to make, overwhelm creeps in & remembering it all can be strenuous. This is important to keep in mind, because when selling, it's crucial that your prospect:
1. Doesn't get overwhelmed with information (too much) and 2. Doesn't feel like your solution is missing something (t oo little) And that's why in marketing messages & presentations, focusing on three points (under a single, overarching idea) is the ultimate sweet spot.

Pattern #3 : The Credibility-Rapport Shift.
As you probably know, an important part of persuasive copy is demonstrating that the expert or company behind the product or service is credible (" why should I listen to you?") . However, often people can fall into the trap of coming off as braggadocious, which is a turn off. How do you avoid that? By countering credibility statements ("look what I've accomplished") with rapport statements ("look where I came from or what I still have, that I'm battling through"). I picked up on this rhythm in the section of the sales letter where Dan is talking about his accomplishments as a form of proof. Almost every time, whenever he makes a credibility statement, he goes right back to rapport. He's constantly shifting between highs and lows.

Pattern #4 : Affinity Fishing.
Dan often talks about the importance of sharing personal characteristics and experiences to connect with others in a deep way. The idea is that something you share about yourself, that may seem insignificant to you, can connect strongly with someone else and form an "affinity bridge", where they k now, like, and trust you more because of the similarity or similarities.
The same could be said for making a prospect believe that a product or service is i deal for them or that something is possible for them.
In the paragraph you're about to read below, Dan describes, in detail, the wide variety of people in his R enegade Millionaire group, including swingers and fly fisherman.
The intent here is that the person reading this will not only think "this applies to everybody", but specifically see a characteristic that makes them think "that's me!". Because overall, you want to create as many opportunities for an "affinity bridge" as possible.
By the way, that doesn't mean just naming off a bunch of different types of people. He specifically says that these are the people a lready in his group (proof).

Pattern #7 : Content-Priming (Copy Disguised As Content).
Few people, other than copywriters, prefer to read a sales letter. The majority would be much happier knowing that what they're consuming is "content", just like in a newspaper or book.
That's why advertorials and magalogs have worked so well, for so long. They don't present themselves as sales letters. Instead, they slip under the radar, appearing innocent in intent.
In Dan's Renegade Millionaire sales letter, he follows this principle, but with a slightly different approach.
He starts with honesty and transparency, saying "this is one of the longest sales letters you'll ever read".
However, he flips the narrative when he gives you three reasons why it's long, most all of which relate to the valuable content inside the sales letter.
Of course, everything in the sales letter was written with intent to sell the product, but Dan frames it in a way that primes the reader to see it as content.
You can do the same by stating upfront what they're going to LEARN inside your sales letter. Make it a goal for you copy to be valuable in and of itself, without sacrificing it's selling power.

Pattern #14 : Mathematical Urgency .
This was a favorite part for many of the members I spoke with about the letter. It's not often that urgency is so mathematical. Usually, if anything, a specific date is given or it's simply mentioned that the offer won't last for long. Overall, urgency is usually quite generic. However, in this case, it's extremely specific and based entirely on the prospect's own specifications. In the example below, Dan uses a current statistic (the average life expectancy in the United States) to have prospects calculate the amount of time they statistically have left to live. As you've probably noticed, Dan is not subtle. Then he asks them questions about what they want to do with that time left, which of course, would be best utilized using his product. This is the most direct and fear-based part of the letter. I certainly wouldn't recommend such an approach for most people. However, a big part of what makes this powerful is how the prospect calculates the number himself and how the calculation is based on a real statistic. So, from a more general perspective, you could create another time equation that mimics the structure. For instance, just riffing here, but let's say we're selling a productivity system at the start of the year where the expert claims to double your productivity. The prospect can be instructed to take their average amount of daily working hours and multiply them by 365. If the hook is that you can get just as much done in half the time, you can then calculate how much time they will have wasted yearly and are wasting daily without the system. Then you can tie the guarantee to this.

Pattern #16 : Perceived Value Equations .
This is a continuation from the Mathematical Urgency pattern. After having prospects calculate the amount of time they may have left to live, he asks that they take that number and multiply it by the percent of " autonomy improvement" that they think could be created in their business life. Then he asks, "if you could buy such hours...how much would you pay for them?" From there, he has them multiply the hours by the price, which creates an insanely large number, which bumps up the perceived value of the product. The idea is to create some type of value equation, related to what's being sold, that is basically rigged to end up with a very high number. This is a powerful way of demonstrating perceived value, particularly because it involves the prospect to figure it out on their own, plus it's mathematics, which is seen as truthful.
